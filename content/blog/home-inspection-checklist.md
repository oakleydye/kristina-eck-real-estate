A home inspection is one of the most important steps in the home buying process. It can save you from costly surprises and give you leverage in negotiations. Here's everything you need to know about home inspections and what to expect.

## Why Home Inspections Matter

Think of a home inspection as a comprehensive physical exam for the house. A professional inspector will evaluate the home's condition, identify potential problems, and estimate remaining life for major systems and components.

### What's at Stake

Without an inspection, you could be buying:
- A roof that needs replacing next year ($15,000-$30,000)
- A failing HVAC system ($5,000-$10,000)
- Foundation issues ($10,000-$100,000+)
- Hidden water damage and mold ($5,000-$50,000)
- Electrical hazards that pose safety risks

**The average home inspection costs $300-$500. Skipping it to save a few hundred dollars could cost you tens of thousands.**

## What Inspectors Check

A standard home inspection covers these major areas:

### 1. Structural Components

**Foundation**
- Cracks, settling, or shifting
- Water intrusion or moisture issues
- Proper drainage around foundation
- Basement or crawl space conditions

**Framing and Structure**
- Wall integrity and plumbness
- Floor levelness and bounce
- Ceiling condition
- Load-bearing walls and beams

**Roof Structure**
- Rafters and trusses
- Decking condition
- Proper ventilation
- Signs of sagging or damage

### 2. Exterior

**Roof and Gutters**
- Shingle condition and age
- Missing or damaged shingles
- Flashing around chimneys and vents
- Gutter function and drainage
- Downspout placement

**Siding and Trim**
- Material condition
- Paint or stain condition
- Wood rot or damage
- Proper installation
- Caulking and seals

**Windows and Doors**
- Operation and seal quality
- Glass condition
- Frame integrity
- Weather stripping
- Locks and hardware

**Grading and Drainage**
- Soil slope away from house
- Standing water issues
- Retaining walls
- Driveway and walkway condition

### 3. Roofing

Age and remaining life are critical:

**Expected Lifespan by Material:**
- Asphalt shingles: 15-30 years
- Metal roofing: 40-70 years
- Tile: 50-100 years
- Slate: 100+ years

**Red Flags:**
- Missing or curling shingles
- Granule loss in gutters
- Daylight visible through attic
- Water stains on ceilings
- Multiple layers of shingles

### 4. Plumbing System

**Water Supply**
- Pipe material and condition
- Water pressure
- Functional flow test
- Main shut-off valve
- Signs of leaks

**Drains, Wastes, and Vents**
- Drain function
- Vent pipe condition
- Sewage line condition
- Septic system (if applicable)

**Water Heater**
- Age and type
- Capacity for home size
- Temperature and pressure relief valve
- Signs of corrosion or leaks
- Proper venting

**Fixtures**
- Faucets, sinks, toilets
- Bathtubs and showers
- Visible leaks or damage

### 5. Electrical System

**Service Panel**
- Amperage (100, 150, or 200 amp)
- Proper labeling
- Circuit breaker condition
- No double-tapped breakers
- Grounding

**Wiring**
- Type of wiring (copper vs. aluminum)
- Condition and age
- Proper installation
- GFCI outlets in wet areas
- Outlet functionality

**Safety Concerns:**
- Outdated knob-and-tube wiring
- Aluminum wiring (fire hazard)
- Overloaded circuits
- DIY modifications
- Missing ground wires

### 6. HVAC Systems

**Heating**
- System type and age
- Operational test
- Heat exchanger condition (furnace)
- Proper venting
- Maintenance history

**Cooling**
- Air conditioner type and age
- Refrigerant levels
- Compressor operation
- Ductwork condition
- Filter condition

**Expected Lifespan:**
- Furnace: 15-20 years
- Air conditioner: 10-15 years
- Heat pump: 10-15 years
- Boiler: 15-30 years

### 7. Interior

**Walls and Ceilings**
- Cracks or damage
- Water stains
- Proper finish
- Drywall condition

**Floors**
- Level and solid
- Damage or wear
- Squeaks or soft spots
- Proper installation

**Doors and Windows**
- Operation
- Seals and locks
- Glass condition
- Frame integrity

**Stairs and Railings**
- Stability and safety
- Handrail presence
- Riser and tread condition
- Code compliance

### 8. Insulation and Ventilation

**Attic**
- Insulation type and R-value
- Proper ventilation
- Soffit and ridge vents
- Moisture or mold
- Pest evidence

**Crawl Space/Basement**
- Vapor barrier presence
- Insulation condition
- Moisture control
- Ventilation

### 9. Appliances (If Included)

Basic operation test of:
- Dishwasher
- Range/oven
- Microwave
- Garbage disposal
- Exhaust fans

## What Inspectors DON'T Check

Understand the limitations:

**Not Included:**
- Septic system (requires specialist)
- Well water quality (separate test)
- Radon levels (separate test)
- Mold testing (separate specialist)
- Pest inspection (separate inspector)
- Pool and spa (may require specialist)
- Inside walls or under floors
- Cosmetic issues

**Consider Additional Inspections For:**
- Older homes (pre-1980)
- Homes with septic/well
- Properties in radon-prone areas
- Signs of pests or mold
- Pools, spas, or outbuildings

## The Inspection Process

### Before the Inspection

**Do:**
- Attend the inspection (highly recommended)
- Ask questions as you go
- Take photos and notes
- Test everything yourself too
- Bring a flashlight and notepad

**Don't:**
- Bring your entire family
- Interfere with the inspector's work
- Make decisions on the spot
- Panic over minor issues

### During the Inspection

**What to Focus On:**
- Big-ticket items (roof, HVAC, foundation)
- Safety hazards
- Items affecting habitability
- Deferred maintenance

**Ask the Inspector:**
- Age and remaining life of major systems
- Estimated repair costs for issues
- What's most urgent vs. what can wait
- Maintenance recommendations
- How issues compare to similar homes

### After the Inspection

You'll receive a detailed report within 24-48 hours covering:
- List of all issues found
- Photos of problems
- Severity ratings
- Recommendations for repairs
- Sometimes estimated costs

## Interpreting the Report

### Understanding Severity

**Major Issues (Must Address):**
- Structural problems
- Roof replacement needed
- Failing HVAC systems
- Electrical hazards
- Plumbing failures
- Foundation issues

**Moderate Issues (Should Address):**
- Aging systems near end of life
- Minor roof repairs
- Drainage improvements
- Window seal failures
- Cosmetic damage

**Minor Issues (Nice to Fix):**
- Caulking and sealing
- Minor repairs
- Maintenance items
- Cosmetic improvements

### What's "Normal"

**Every House Has Issues**

Even new construction will have items on the inspection report. What matters is:

- Severity of issues
- Cost to repair
- Safety concerns
- Age of home vs. issues found

**Red Flags:**
- Seller hiding or concealing issues
- Multiple major systems at end of life
- Poor maintenance throughout
- Safety hazards
- Unpermitted additions or work

## After the Inspection: Your Options

### 1. Request Repairs

Ask the seller to fix specific issues before closing:

**Best For:**
- Safety hazards
- Code violations
- Major system failures
- Structural issues

**Typical Seller Response:**
- Agree to specific repairs
- Hire licensed contractors
- Provide receipts and warranties

### 2. Request Credit

Ask for money off the price to handle repairs yourself:

**Pros:**
- You control the repairs
- Choose your own contractors
- Can shop for better prices
- Repairs done to your standards

**Cons:**
- More work for you
- Have to coordinate after closing
- May not get full repair cost

### 3. Negotiate Price Reduction

Lower the purchase price to account for needed repairs:

**When to Consider:**
- Multiple big-ticket items
- Seller unwilling to repair
- You prefer to handle improvements
- Issues discovered late in process

### 4. Walk Away

If your contract has an inspection contingency, you can cancel and get your earnest money back:

**Valid Reasons:**
- Deal-breaker issues discovered
- Repair costs exceed budget
- Seller unwilling to negotiate
- Better opportunities available

## Negotiation Strategies

### What to Ask For

**Focus On:**
- Health and safety issues
- Anything not working
- Major system failures
- Structural problems
- Issues that make home unlivable

**Don't Sweat:**
- Minor cosmetic issues
- Normal wear and tear
- Maintenance items you can DIY
- Personal preference items

### Making the Request

**Be Reasonable:**
- Prioritize the big stuff
- Don't nickel and dime over minor items
- Consider the home's age and price
- Look at comparable homes

**Present Professionally:**
- Written request with specific issues
- Include contractor estimates if possible
- Explain why each matters
- Offer compromise options

## Special Considerations

### Older Homes

Homes over 50 years old commonly have:
- Outdated electrical (60-amp service)
- Cast iron plumbing
- Asbestos siding or insulation
- Lead paint
- Settling and shifting

**Know what you're getting into and budget accordingly.**

### Foreclosures and Short Sales

Banks sell "as-is" but you should still inspect:
- Identify major issues
- Budget for repairs
- Negotiate price accordingly
- Expect deferred maintenance

### New Construction

Yes, inspect new homes too!

**Common Issues:**
- Installation mistakes
- Missing insulation
- Improper grading
- Unfinished punch list items
- Code violations

**Builder warranties don't cover everything, and issues caught before closing are easier to fix.**

## Choosing an Inspector

### Qualifications to Look For

- Licensed (if required in your state)
- ASHI (American Society of Home Inspectors) certified
- At least 5 years experience
- Professional liability insurance
- Good online reviews

### Questions to Ask

1. How long have you been inspecting?
2. How many inspections have you done?
3. What's included in your inspection?
4. When will I get the report?
5. Can I attend the inspection?
6. What does your report look like?
7. Do you have references?

### Red Flags

- Lowest price (you get what you pay for)
- Rushes through inspection
- Downplays issues
- Has relationship with realtor that feels too cozy
- Recommends specific contractors
- No references or credentials

## Cost and Timing

### Typical Costs

- Standard inspection: $300-$500
- Large home (3,500+ sq ft): $500-$700
- Radon test: $150-$300
- Septic inspection: $300-$600
- Termite/pest: $100-$300
- Mold testing: $300-$900

### Timeline

- Inspection clause: 7-10 days typically
- Schedule inspection: Within 3-5 days of accepted offer
- Receive report: 24-48 hours after inspection
- Review and decide: 1-2 days
- Submit request to seller: Before deadline
